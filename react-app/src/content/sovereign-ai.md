On February 28, 2026, the United States launched Operation Epic Fury - joint strikes with Israel on Iranian IRGC facilities, air defenses, and missile sites. It was the first time the US had deployed LUCAS autonomous drones in combat. 

Hours before the bombs dropped, President Trump had ordered all federal agencies to **immediately stop using Anthropic's Claude**. Defense Secretary Hegseth had just designated Anthropic a Supply Chain Risk (SCR) to national security - a label typically reserved for Chinese companies like Huawei. And yet, CENTCOM used Claude anyway, running intelligence assessments and battle scenario simulations during the operation.

That contradiction tells you everything about where American AI strategy stands today.

## The 72 Hours That Exposed Everything

The breakdown started in July 2025, when the DoD's (now DoW) Chief Digital and AI Office awarded Anthropic a two-year, $200M prototype contract. Anthropic became the first AI company to deploy large language models on classified Pentagon networks. But the contract came with Anthropic's own red lines: Claude could not be used for mass domestic surveillance of US citizens or to direct autonomous lethal weapons systems.

The Pentagon, under Hegseth, demanded those restrictions be removed. They wanted an "any lawful use" clause - full military discretion over how the model gets deployed. Anthropic refused. The standoff escalated fast. On February 27, Trump ordered agencies to cut off Anthropic immediately. The next day, Hegseth designated the company a supply chain risk, meaning any contractor doing business with the US military could no longer engage commercially with Anthropic.

Then, within hours of that designation, OpenAI announced its own $200M Pentagon deal to supply AI to classified networks claiming it had more guardrails than any previous classified AI deployment, including Anthropic's. Altman himself later acknowledged the optics, admitting the timing made the deal look opportunistic and sloppy. The guardrails OpenAI cited still relied on all lawful purposes language - the exact clause Anthropic had rejected. Meanwhile, the QuitGPT movement erupted: ChatGPT uninstall rates surged 295% in the days following the deal. Over 1.5 million users reportedly cancelled their subscriptions by early March, many migrating to Claude - the same model that had just been banned by the government.

The irony writes itself.

## The Absurdity of Asking Permission

Here is the problem: in the middle of an active war, the US military's access to its most capable AI tools has been contingent on a contract dispute with a private company in San Francisco.

The procurement loop for government AI currently works like this: negotiate terms with a private vendor, get legal approval, get congressional funding, wait for a contracting officer, and hope the company's ethics board doesn't veto the use case mid-deployment. For enterprise software, that's annoying. For wartime operations, it's a national security liability.

Anthropic's principles are genuinely defensible. Mass domestic surveillance and autonomous killing machines are things reasonable people should resist. But the structural problem isn't whether Anthropic was right or wrong but rather that the US government has built its classified AI infrastructure on a foundation it doesn't control and cannot override. A private company holds a veto card over tools being used in active combat. That is not a sustainable arrangement for any serious fighting force.

OpenAI stepping in doesn't solve this. Corporate AI companies are businesses with shareholders, public-facing brands, and user bases that generate political pressure. The QuitGPT numbers prove it: 1.5 million users don't cancel subscriptions over abstract policy debates. The moment military use becomes a liability for a company's consumer business, the calculus changes. Dependency on private vendors means dependency on their incentives.

## What Sovereign AI Actually Means

The answer isn't to ban private AI from defense. It's to stop being **dependent** on it.

Sovereign AI means the government owns and controls its own model stack. No private company can pull the plug. No ethics board can veto a use case at 2am during an airstrike. The government has done this before: the nuclear program, GPS, ARPANET. These were treated as national strategic assets too important to outsource entirely to private contractors.

The gap between open-source and closed-source AI is not just about raw model weights. Companies like **Poetiq AI** have shown that a well-engineered reasoning harness layered on top of existing open-source models can dramatically close the performance gap. Their meta-system recently set SOTA on ARC-AGI-2, the most demanding fluid reasoning benchmark in the field, at 54% accuracy, surpassing Google Gemini Deep Think's previous best of 45%, at less than half the cost per problem. With their harness applied, model accuracy climbs from ~53% to 75%, clearing the human baseline of 60%. 

The insight is this: **the intelligence is in the reasoning infrastructure, not just the weights**. A sovereign open-source stack with the right reasoning harness on top can match proprietary performance for the well-defined, structured decision tasks that military operations actually require — target assessment, logistics optimization, intelligence synthesis. You don't need GPT-5 to do these well. You need a model you own, running proprietary reasoning pipelines you control.

## The Problem: America Is Losing the Open-Source Race

This vision has an uncomfortable prerequisite. The US is behind in the open-source model race.

Chinese open-source models have shocked the world in the past two years delivering frontier performance at a fraction of the training cost and openly releasing weights that the rest of the world can build on. The US lead in AI has historically been held by a handful of private companies. That's not the same as a national capability.

China doesn't have the Anthropic problem. The Chinese government has direct access to its frontier AI infrastructure. PLA-adjacent labs are not being constrained by consumer ethics boards or 295% uninstall spikes. **In a fast-moving conflict, that asymmetry matters enormously.** Relying on OpenAI or Anthropic to be responsible military AI contractors is the equivalent of outsourcing F-35 production and hoping the vendor's ethics committee approves the weapons package.

Winning the next war requires a sovereign AI capability that can move at the speed of operations, not at the speed of Congress.

## The Clock Is Ticking

The Anthropic standoff will be studied in policy schools for years. But the lesson isn't about Anthropic, or OpenAI, or Pete Hegseth. The lesson is that the US built a critical national security capability on a foundation it doesn't own, and it got exposed the moment that foundation wobbled.

The window to fix this isn't infinite. Every month spent in contract disputes is a month the open-source gap widens. Every dependency on a private vendor is a potential veto card held against the government during the moments that matter most.

The path forward is clear: fund sovereign model infrastructure the way the country once funded the nuclear program and the internet. Build the reasoning harnesses on top of open source models. **Stop asking permission to use your own tools in your own wars.**

The US doesn't negotiate with adversaries over which weapons it gets to use. It shouldn't have to negotiate with AI companies either.
